Mass surveillance technology has no place in our society.

Despite the risks to our safety and human rights, facial surveillance is already being deployed by police, pubs, stores and more. Our faces are being scanned, tracked and identified, often without our knowledge.​

We need to ban facial surviellance and stop mass surveillance. Here’s why:

➜ It’s incompatible with democracy

Facial surveillance is unlike other forms of surveillance we have ever experienced before. When widely implemented, it enables automated and ubiquitous mass monitoring of entire groups of people, and it’s nearly impossible to avoid.

Research shows [REF] that pervasive surveillance is a highly efficient way to enforce social order—it encourages us to conform and creates a punitive culture of compliance.

We know people change their behaviour when they feel they are being watched [REF], this makes it harder to engage in public debate, organise and protest, and ultimately hold people in power accountable.

➜ It undermines our human rights

Facial surveillance inherently undermines our right to privacy. Everyone should be free to move through the world without their movements and behaviours being constantly tracked and monitored.

It also undermines other rights, like freedom of association and assembly, and freedom of expression and opinion. These rights are fundamental to a thriving democracy.

Because of bias and inaccuraces [REF], facial surveillance undermines the right to equality or non-discrimination.

When used by law enforcement, facial surveillance can undermine the right not to be subject to arbitrary arrest or detention. There are already examples of people being wrongfully arrested due to facial surveillance overseas [REF].

Because of the lack of transparency about how some facial surveillance systems function and how they arrive at an outcome, facial surveillance can also undermine the right to equality before the law and to a fair trial.

➜ It’s dangerous

Our face data is incredibly sensitive. When a facial surveillance system captures your face, it creates a unique biometric faceprint [REF].

Once companies and governments collect and store our biometric data, it puts all of us at heightened risk of harm in instances of hacks or breaches.

Unlike your password, you can’t easily change your face once the data falls into the wrong hands.

If you were concerned about the Optus data breach, imagine how much worse it would be if it included your unique faceprint too.

➜ It’s biased and unjust

Research shows that facial recognition technology is riddled with errors, with lower accuracy for women and people with darker skin tones [REF].

When facial surveillance gets it wrong it creates real world harm, like harassment and wrongful imprisonment. There have already been at least three wrongful arrests in the US based on law enforcement bias and inaccurate technology [REF]. Do we have to wait for innocent people to be harmed by facial surveillance in Australia before we ban it?

Facial surveillance regularly misidentifies People of Colour, women, gender non conforming people, and children. This means it worsens pre-existing inequalities, fuels discrimination at scale, and is a dangerous threat to some of the most marginalised or vulnerable in our communities

➜ It’s oppressive

Facial surveillance is oppressive by design.

There are already immense power imbalances and information asymmetries between those in power and the rest of us. Facial surveillance worsens these imbalances, and tilts the scale further in favour of the already-powerful.

➜ It’s spreading.

Facial surveillance is already being used around Australia, without our consent or a change to have a public debate.

Police, shopping centers and retail stores, pubs, stadiums, and schools are all increasingly using facial surveillance.

If facial surveillance technology continues to be developed and deployed, a formidable infrastructure will be built around it and we will be stuck with it.
