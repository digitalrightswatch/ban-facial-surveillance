What do we mean by ‘facial surveillance’?

We use the term ‘facial surveillance’ to mean facial recognition technology (FRT) that enables mass surveillance.

Specifically, this means FRT that implements a ‘one-to-many’ matching or ‘facial identification’. This is when software compares a faceprint of an unknown person with a large database of known faces to determine the identity of the unknown person.

One-to-many identification or real-time use of facial recognition technology is the most uniquely dangerous mass surveillance mechanism invented to date.

This is different to ‘one-to-one’ matching, or ‘facial verification’, which is used to check that someone’s face data matches their identity. For example, the job of facial recognition in biometric passport scanners at airports is to check that you are the same person as who is on your passport — by checking if your face data matches what’s on record. Similarly, if you unlock an iPhone using your faceprint, this is one-to-one matching, and your face data doesn’t leave your device.

While there are still risks involved in one-to-one matching, it is much harder to develop mass surveillance capability through this form of FRT, so we do not include it under the term ‘facial surveillance’. 

Why do we need an outright ban?

Regulation of facial surveillance is not enough—we need a categorical ban.

Facial surveillance poses a fundamental threat to our most basic rights, liberties and safety. It is uniquely dangerous because it, by its nature, enables real-time, mass surveillance of whole populations. It is a fundamental invasion of privacy that puts our freedom of expression and democratic participation at risk.

Facial surveillance is oppressive by design. It exacerbates existing inequalities and power imbalances. This is made worse by the fact that current facial recognition technology is woefully biased and inaccurate.

Facial surveillance is dangerous when it works, and dangerous when it doesn’t.

These threats far outweigh hypothetical future benefits. We should not bet on hypothetical or speculative positive use-cases of facial surveillance, because what we’re gambling with is our rights, safety, and ultimately, our democracy.

We know that even with regulation, law enforcement agencies routinely access personal data without legal authority, and fail to follow data protection rules. We also know that common safeguards such as limiting access to certain groups inevitably expands through shifting political will.

Creating rules that define spaces in which it’s deemed “okay” to spy on everyday Australians is not a win. We must interrogate the difference between reforms that allow the development, use and proliferation of facial surveillance, and steps that actually stop its use, spread and harm.

In a world of data breaches, misuse of power by law enforcement agencies, and racial bias in machine learning algorithms, we cannot afford to embed these technologies in our lives. A ban on the development and deployment of biometric surveillance technology is necessary.

We don’t want to be planning regulatory whack-a-mole. A categorical ban on facial surveillance is the only way to prevent the proliferation of this dangerous technology.  

Is there a difference between commercial or public use of facial surveillance?

When it comes to facial surveillance, there is no meaningful difference between commercial and public use.

Commercial use of surveillance technology and its application in law enforcement are so intertwined, legislation cannot meaningfully pull them apart. For example, government agencies regularly procure and use commercial technology, or compel the private sector to provide access to data.

Recent changes to tech law and policy in Australia have encouraged commercial companies to gather and retain more data for the purpose of facilitating data transfer to law enforcement agencies, at the expense of our right to privacy.

Would Australia be the first to ban facial surveillance?

A growing number of cities and countries around the world are aware of the unique harms posed by facial recognition technology and have already taken steps to prevent its proliferation.

Seventeen cities across the US have already banned Facial Recognition Technology, including:

  • Virginia bans police use of facial recognition technology (2021)

  • Minneapolis vote to ban facial recognition technology (2021)

  • Portland ban facial recognition technology use by city bureaus (2020)

  • Boston ban facial recognition technology (2020)

  • San Francisco ban facial recognition technology (2020)

And sentiment against facial surveillance is growing:

  • European Parliament calls for a ban on facial recognition (2022)

  • US Bill to ban federal use of facial recognition in immigration and law enforcement (2021)

Many of these bans are a result of human rights organisations and civil society groups around the world alerting the public to the dangers of facial surveillance and campaigning to put a stop to it. Groups in the UK, Europe, and the US are all calling for a ban on this dangerous surveillance technology.
